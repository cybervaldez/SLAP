The Problem

Every AI page looks the same.

Purple gradients. Inter font. Rounded pill buttons. A hero section that says "Revolutionize Your Workflow" in 48px bold, followed by a paragraph nobody reads, followed by two buttons -- one filled, one ghost.

The AI doesn't lack vocabulary. It lacks taste. It reaches for the most statistically likely design pattern and produces something that is technically correct, aesthetically generic, and emotionally bankrupt.

We call it slop. Not because it's broken. Because it could belong to anyone.

If your landing page could belong to any competitor, it belongs to none of them.
The Discovery

One page. Three lenses. Nine costumes.

One page. Unedited AI copy. Nine radically different design treatments. Five expert critics scoring every section. The result was a controlled experiment in how much design actually matters.

1
The Performer
AI Copy, Unedited

We asked AI to write a landing page, a product catalog, and a long-form article. We didn't edit a single word. The copy arrived proud of itself -- full of synergy, disruption, and artisanal hand-crafted word salad.

2
The Critics
Five Expert Personas

Marketing, UX, Product, Technical, and Design experts each scored the same content. They disagreed constantly. A 9/10 from the brand expert was a 3/10 from the engineer. The tension was the finding.

3
Redemption
Can Design Rescue Content?

Same words, nine costumes. We dressed the copy in Brutalist severity, Art Deco glamour, Memphis chaos, and six others. Scores shifted by as much as 6 points. Design didn't just change how it looked. It changed how it read.

The Tension

Design is a lens, not a bandage.

Brutalist
Exposes

Strips away polish, forces the reader to confront hollow words naked. There is nowhere to hide when the frame is raw concrete and the type is screaming in uppercase. Bad content doesn't just look bad here -- it looks dishonest.

Warm Organic
Hides

Wraps clichés in soft textures until they feel like wisdom. Rounded corners, earthy tones, and generous whitespace give even the emptiest phrases a sense of groundedness. The design is doing all the emotional labor the words refused to do.

Art Deco
Raises Expectations

Gilded framing makes mediocre copy feel like a broken promise. When the border is gold leaf and the typography is Gatsby-era grandeur, the reader expects substance. The gap between presentation and content becomes the critique itself.

This is the core finding. Design is a lens, not a bandage.
