SLAP!

The Anti-Slop Design Study

What happens when you let AI write your website... and then roast it

The Problem

Every AI page looks the same.

You have seen this page. The confident headline that says nothing. Three benefit cards with icons that could be anything. A testimonial from someone whose title sounds invented. A gradient button begging you to "Get Started." It is the default output of every AI writing tool, every template marketplace, every "ship fast" tutorial. Not bad, exactly. Just deeply, terminally familiar.

The Discovery

We built the worst page on purpose.

Prompted an AI for a professional landing page. Accepted every default. Shipped the cliches as-is. Then we put it through three layers of structured criticism.

1

The Performer

AI copy, unedited, proud of itself. Every buzzword intact. Every call-to-action gleaming with false urgency.

2

The Critics

Five expert personas score every section. They disagree on almost everything except what is mediocre.

3

Redemption

Can design rescue doomed content? We dressed the same words in nine different aesthetics to find out.

The Proof

Same words. Nine designs. Scores from 2 to 8.

  • SLAP! 2.4 baseline
  • Brutalist 6.2 raw exposure
  • Neo-Minimal 7.1 quiet authority
  • Art Deco 5.8 gilded tension
  • Swiss 7.4 structured clarity
  • Glassmorphism 4.5 frosted distraction
  • Memphis 5.0 playful deflection
  • Scandinavian 6.8 warm concealment
  • Warm Organic 6.0 comfortable hiding

Identical content. Wildly different reactions.

The Tension

Design can't save bad content.

But it can expose or elevate it.

Brutalist exposes — strips away polish, leaves the words nowhere to hide. Every cliche stands in the open.

Warm Organic hides — wraps cliches in comfort. Rounded corners and earth tones make mediocrity feel like a hug.

Art Deco raises expectations — gilded framing, broken promise. The higher the aesthetic standard, the worse generic copy performs.

Author Notes

How we almost ruined it by being too funny.

The first version of this study was a comedy routine. Every heading had a punchline. Every data point came with a callback. The personas roasted each other in footnotes. Readers laughed, skimmed, and retained nothing.

We rewrote it straight. Let the content failures be the humor. Turns out, a landing page that calls itself "the future of workflow synergy" is already funnier than anything we could add. The slapstick was always in the source material.

We stopped trying to be funny and started letting the evidence be absurd.
